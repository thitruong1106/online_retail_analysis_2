# Key Findings

The goal of this analysis was to understand revenue patterns, return behaviour, product performance, and customer dynamics within the business.

---

## 1. Revenue Trends: A Holiday-Driven Business

When looking through the two-year period, one pattern stands out above everything else: the business runs on Christmas.

During this period, revenue climbs steadily in September, builds through October, and peaks in November at approximately **£1.47 million in 2010** and **£1.5 million in November 2011**. When January arrives, revenue drops sharply.

This is not surprising for a retailer selling decorative items, giftware, and home accessories. Customers are mainly buying Christmas presents, stocking fillers, and seasonal decorations. The pattern during this seasonal period is clear and consistent across both years.

However, this level of seasonal dependence creates real business challenges. Cash flow can become unpredictable, with strong months followed by quiet ones. Inventory planning is crucial. Too much stock after the holidays means money tied up in products that nobody is interested in during February. Operational pressure also intensifies dramatically during peak months, stretching staff and systems.

The question is whether the business can find ways to drive revenue in other months without losing the strength of the holiday peak.

---

## 2. Return Behaviour: What Looked Like a Problem Was Actually Accounting

At first glance, the return data was concerning, which is why further investigation was necessary. Return rates jumped from around **11% in December 2010 to 20% in January 2011**, and from **December 2011 to January 2012 they reached approximately 32%**. My initial assumption was that customers were returning unwanted Christmas gifts in large numbers.

After further investigation, one product explained the reason for the unusually high return rates: **StockCode 23166**, described as *Medium Ceramic Top Storage Jar*, which showed **74,215 units returned in January 2011 alone**.

When I examined the invoices containing these returns, they were not spread across hundreds of different customers but instead appeared within a single distinct invoice containing extremely large quantities.

This pattern does not match normal consumer behaviour but instead indicates a **bulk operational reversal or wholesale order cancellation**.

When initially looking at monthly return rates, I suspected that customers might have been unhappy with their purchases or that the return policy was too generous. Neither assumption turned out to be correct. The January 2011 spike was actually an **accounting or operational adjustment**, not a reflection of customer sentiment.

---

## 3. Customer Behaviour: Loyal Customers Drive the Business

A small group of customers accounts for a large share of revenue.

The **top 10% of customers generate nearly 64% of all revenue**.  
The **top 1% accounts for almost 32%**, and the **top 0.1% (approximately 6 customers) contributes more than 12% of total revenue**.

This is a clear example of the **Pareto principle**, where revenue is concentrated among a small group of high-value customers. When just six customers represent more than a tenth of the company’s revenue, the business becomes vulnerable to their individual circumstances.

If even one of these customers switched suppliers or stopped purchasing, the impact would be immediate and measurable.

The implication is not that the business should ignore other customers, but that retention of these high-value customers should be intentional and personal. Automated email campaigns cannot replace the value of a relationship manager building a real connection. Knowing who these customers are, understanding their needs, and ensuring they feel valued should be a priority.

At the same time, understanding what makes these customers different—whether they are wholesalers, repeat gift buyers, or something else—could reveal opportunities to attract more customers like them.

---

## 4. Product Performance

The products driving revenue and volume are largely what we would expect from a gift and homeware retailer.

In terms of revenue, **Regency Cake Stand 3 Tier**, **White Hanging Heart T-Light Holder**, **Jumbo Bag Red Retrospot**, and **Party Bunting** appear among the top products generating revenue for the business.

By units sold, the list includes **World War 2 Gliders Assorted Design**, **Assorted Colour Bird Ornament**, along with the **White Hanging Heart T-Light Holder** and **Jumbo Bag Red Retrospot**.

These are the types of products people commonly purchase as gifts or to decorate their homes for special occasions.

These products form the backbone of the business, particularly during peak seasons. Stock availability should therefore be carefully managed in the lead-up to the fourth quarter. Stock shortages in November could result in missed revenue opportunities.

---

## 5. Product Return Analysis: Seasonal Items Carry Higher Risk

When products were first ranked by return rate, the results looked unusual. Several products showed return rates exceeding **100%**, which is mathematically impossible for normal consumer returns.

This turned out to be the result of operational reversals appearing again in the data, combined with products that had extremely low sales volumes where a single return could significantly distort the percentage.

After filtering out products with fewer than **50 units sold**, a clearer picture emerged. The products with the highest return rates were largely **seasonal decorative items**, including cherry lights, festive candles, and holiday decorations.

This is not surprising, as seasonal products have a short selling window and are more likely to be returned after the peak period. In many cases, these returns may reflect normal seasonal buying patterns or unsold stock rather than product quality issues.

This suggests that higher return rates for seasonal items may be an expected part of the business model, though return reasons should still be monitored to distinguish between normal seasonal behaviour and genuine product problems.

---

## Conclusion

This retailer has a clear and successful formula: selling appealing gift and homeware products to customers who are most active during the Christmas shopping period. The revenue figures clearly demonstrate that this strategy works.

However, the analysis also reveals several vulnerabilities. The business is heavily dependent on a small number of customers and a narrow seasonal window. The January return spikes, while not indicative of customer dissatisfaction, still represent operational complexity that must be managed carefully. Seasonal products, while profitable, also carry higher return rates that should be considered in planning.

Future analysis could explore where customers are located geographically, whether they return repeatedly, how different product categories perform, and whether marketing activity influences purchasing behaviour.

For now, the priorities are clear: **protect the holiday peak, retain high-value customers, and ensure operational adjustments are not mistaken for customer behaviour.**
